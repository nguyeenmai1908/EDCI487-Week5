What Is a Complex Adaptive System?
~8 minutes
By the end of this section, you will be able to
  • Recognize a feedback loop using a concrete example (the ice-albedo loop)
  • Explain how Talukder's (2024) framework applies systems thinking to climate and health
  • Name the six core properties of a complex adaptive system, using examples you've already seen

You can roughly predict the weather next week, but not exactly. Not because we lack the tools — because the system has too many moving parts that all affect each other.

Here is one example of how that works: Arctic ice melts → darker ocean absorbs more heat → more ice melts.?Ice is white — it bounces sunlight back into space. Ocean water is dark — it soaks that heat up instead. So when ice melts, the surface absorbs more energy, which melts even more ice. Nobody planned that loop. It just happens — the system responds to itself. That is a complex adaptive system in miniature.

Climate change is full of loops like this. They overlap, they push and pull on each other, and they do not point to a single cause or a single fix.

Talukder (2024) applies this same thinking to human health — showing how poverty, pollution, and inequality feed into each other the same way ice and ocean heat do. The system is different, but the shape is the same.

As you read, watch for the loops — places where one change triggers another that circles back to where it started.

As you read, look for

Three things to spot in the text

  1. 01 Find one loop or ripple effect in the health framework — something that feeds back into itself, the way the ice-albedo example did. What is it?
  2. 02 The framework emphasizes social determinants alongside ecological ones. How does this connect to what you learned in Week 3 about climate justice?
  3. 03 What would a systems approach to teaching look like in K–12? What might you have to give up from a more traditional lesson structure?

Naming the Pattern

You just saw a complex adaptive system at work. Here are the ideas behind it.

The ice-albedo loop, and the patterns you spotted in Talukder's framework, are both examples of the same six properties. You don't need to memorize these — just recognize them when they show up.

🔄
Feedback Loops
Outputs become inputs. Like the ice-albedo loop above — the system responds to itself.
🌿
Emergence
Outcomes arise from interactions, not from any single cause. No one designed a refugee crisis — it emerged from a system.
⚖️
Non-linearity
Small changes can produce large effects. Large interventions can produce surprisingly small ones. Cause and effect aren't proportional.
🔗
Interdependence
Components affect each other across boundaries — ecological, social, economic, political. You can't change one part without touching others.
🧩
Adaptation
The system responds to change. Governments, markets, communities adjust their behaviour — creating both resilience and new vulnerabilities.
⚡
Tipping Points
Gradual pressure can produce sudden, irreversible shifts. The system crosses a threshold and reorganizes into a different state.

The system is not broken. It is functioning as designed — just in ways that are no longer safe. Next: the planetary boundaries that define what "safe" means.
