Teaching with Complexity
~8 minutes
By the end of this section, you will be able to
  • Describe four design principles for teaching complex systems in K–12
  • Evaluate a real classroom example (Schaeffer, 2023) against those principles
  • Identify where interdisciplinary climate teaching might be easy or hard to implement in a BC classroom

Understanding climate as a complex system is one thing. Teaching it that way is another. Most classrooms still split knowledge into separate subjects with tidy answers — but complex problems don't work like that.

An interdisciplinary approach means designing learning that matches the shape of the problem — connecting science, social studies, ethics, and students' own experience in one inquiry.

Schaeffer's (2023) Edutopia article, linked at the end of this section, shows what this looks like in practice. Read it through the four principles below.

Instructional Design

Four principles for teaching complex systems

01 💡
Start with a driving question, not a topic
Topics fragment knowledge. Questions invite inquiry across boundaries. A strong driving question is genuinely open, locally relevant, and impossible to answer from within a single subject.
"Why does our neighbourhood look the way it does — and who decides that?"
02 🗺️
Map the system before teaching about it
Before students can understand the system, they need to see it. Systems mapping — drawing the connections, feedback loops, and actors — is itself a learning activity that builds the foundation for everything that follows.
Who grows our food? Who eats first when there's not enough? What happens to the soil?
03 ⚡
Build in tension and productive discomfort
Complex systems do not resolve neatly. Lessons that end with a tidy answer may be teaching the wrong thing. Students need practice sitting with uncertainty, ambiguity, and competing values.
"What should we do?" when the right answer depends on who you ask.
04 🌍
Connect local and planetary scales
Students can lose themselves in global abstraction, or disengage from problems that feel too big to touch. Strong units anchor planetary dynamics in local examples — the watershed, the schoolyard, the neighbourhood — and then zoom out.
Start with the tree on your street. End at the planetary boundary for land-system change.
