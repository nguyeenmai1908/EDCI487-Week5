### Body text

Now we zoom out. Not to abandon what we found there, but to ask a different question: **why is the system that produces these harms so difficult to change?**

## SECTION 2 — What Is a Complex Adaptive System? (`w5-section2.html`)

**Section label:** Section 2 of 5
**Title:** What Is a Complex Adaptive System?
**Time:** ~8 minutes
**Progress bar start:** 20%

**By the end of this section, you will be able to:**
- Recognize a feedback loop using a concrete example (the ice-albedo loop)
- Explain how Talukder's (2024) framework applies systems thinking to climate and health
- Name the six core properties of a complex adaptive system, using examples you've already seen

---

### Body text — concrete example first

Think about weather. You can spot patterns and make general predictions, but you can't say exactly what will happen in your city three weeks from now. Not because the science is bad — because the system is too interconnected, too responsive, too *alive* to reduce to a simple equation.

Climate works the same way — and here's a small, concrete piece of it: **Arctic ice melts → less white surface to reflect sunlight → more heat absorbed by darker ocean water → more ice melts.** No one designed that loop. No single cause is fully "to blame." The system is responding to itself, and the response feeds back into the system. That's a complex adaptive system in miniature.

Climate change, at the planetary scale, is full of loops like this — interacting, overlapping, sometimes cancelling each other out, sometimes amplifying. No single cause, no single driver, no single fix.

---

### Body text — introducing Talukder's framework

Talukder's (2024) framework takes this same systems lens and applies it to something closer to the classroom: **human health.** It shows how social determinants, environmental exposures, and systemic inequities interact — much like the ice-albedo loop above, but with people's lives at stake instead of sea ice.

As you read, try looking for the *pattern* as much as the content — notice where something seems to respond to itself, or where one change ripples into an area that seems unrelated at first.

### CAS Properties Grid (forest background) — naming what you just saw

**Label:** Naming the Pattern
**Heading:** You just saw a complex adaptive system at work. Here's the vocabulary for it.

*Intro line above the grid:* The ice-albedo loop, and the patterns you spotted in Talukder's framework, are both examples of the same six properties. You don't need to memorize these — just recognize them when they show up.

| Icon | Name | Description |
|------|------|-------------|
| 🔄 | Feedback Loops | Outputs become inputs. Like the ice-albedo loop above — the system responds to itself. |
| 🌿 | Emergence | Outcomes arise from interactions, not from any single cause. No one designed a refugee crisis — it emerged from a system. |
| ⚖️ | Non-linearity | Small changes can produce large effects. Large interventions can produce surprisingly small ones. Cause and effect aren't proportional. |
| 🔗 | Interdependence | Components affect each other across boundaries — ecological, social, economic, political. You can't change one part without touching others. |
| 🧩 | Adaptation | The system responds to change. Governments, markets, communities adjust their behaviour — creating both resilience and new vulnerabilities. |
| ⚡ | Tipping Points | Gradual pressure can produce sudden, irreversible shifts. The system crosses a threshold and reorganizes into a different state. |

### Body text (cream background)

Notice the ozone layer and aerosol loading — in the diagram, both wedges sit entirely inside the green. Global cooperation in the 1980s — through the Montreal Protocol — brought ozone back into the safe zone. It is the clearest example we have of a boundary actually being pulled back from the edge. It is also proof that systemic change is possible.

The planetary boundaries framework is not a counsel of despair. It is a **systems map**. And systems maps tell you where to look, what to connect, and what levers might matter. That is precisely what we need educators to be able to do.

## SECTION 4 — Teaching with Complexity (`w5-section4.html`)

**Section label:** Section 4 of 5
**Title:** Teaching with Complexity
**Time:** ~8 minutes
**Progress bar start:** 60%

**By the end of this section, you will be able to:**

- Evaluate a real classroom example (Schaeffer, 2023) against those principles
- Identify where interdisciplinary climate teaching might be easy or hard to implement in a BC classroom

---

### Body text

Understanding climate as a complex system is one thing. Teaching it that way is another. The dominant model in K–12 education — single subjects, discrete units, clearly bounded answers — is structurally mismatched with the problem it is trying to address.

An interdisciplinary approach is not about adding more content. It is about **designing learning that reflects the actual shape of the problem** — connecting science, social studies, ethics, arts, and students' own lived experience within a single inquiry.

Schaeffer's (2023) Edutopia article — required reading, linked at the end of this section — shows what this looks like in practice: how teachers can build units that cross subject lines, centre student voice, and develop the systems thinking skills students will need as citizens, not just test-takers. The four principles below are the lens to read it through.

---

### Four Design Principles (forest background)

**Label:** Instructional Design
**Heading:** Four principles for teaching complex systems

---

**Principle 1 — Start with a driving question, not a topic**

Topics fragment knowledge. Questions invite inquiry across boundaries. A strong driving question is genuinely open, locally relevant, and impossible to answer from within a single subject.

*Example: "Why does our neighbourhood look the way it does — and who decides that?"*

---

**Principle 2 — Map the system before teaching about it**

Before students can understand the system, they need to see it. Systems mapping — drawing the connections, feedback loops, and actors — is itself a learning activity that builds the vocabulary for everything that follows.

*Example: Who grows our food? Who eats first when there's not enough? What happens to the soil?*

---

**Principle 3 — Build in tension and productive discomfort**

Complex systems do not resolve neatly. Lessons that end with a tidy answer may be teaching the wrong thing. Students need practice sitting with uncertainty, ambiguity, and competing values — not just finding the right answer.

*Example: "What should we do?" when the right answer depends on who you ask.*

---

**Principle 4 — Connect local and planetary scales**

Students can lose themselves in global abstraction, or disengage from problems that feel too big to touch. Strong units anchor planetary dynamics in local examples — the watershed, the schoolyard, the neighbourhood — and then zoom out.

*Example: Start with the tree on your street. End at the planetary boundary for land-system change.*
