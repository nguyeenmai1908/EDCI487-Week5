Planetary Boundaries
~10 minutes
By the end of this section, you will be able to
  • Identify the nine planetary boundaries and which seven have been crossed as of the 2025 assessment
  • Explain the concept of a "safe operating space" for humanity
  • Connect the planetary boundaries framework to the CAS properties from Section 2, using your own example

In 2009, scientists at the Stockholm Resilience Centre proposed a framework for what makes our planet habitable: nine planetary boundaries that define a safe operating space for humanity. Before we look at where things stand, take a guess.

Predict, then reveal

How many do you think we've crossed?

The nine boundaries include things like climate change, biodiversity, freshwater, ocean acidification, and the ozone layer.

Of the nine planetary boundaries, how many do you think humanity has currently crossed?

No wrong answers here — your guess is the point. Commit before you click.

7 of 9 boundaries have been crossed.

The Nine Planetary Boundaries

Where Earth's systems stand — 2025 assessment

⚠ Exceeded Climate Change
⚠ Exceeded Biosphere Integrity (biodiversity loss)
⚠ Exceeded Land-System Change
⚠ Exceeded Freshwater Change
⚠ Exceeded Biogeochemical Flows (nitrogen & phosphorus)
⚠ Exceeded Novel Entities (chemicals, plastics)
⚠ Exceeded Ocean Acidification (newly crossed, 2025)
✓ Safe Stratospheric Ozone Depletion
✓ Safe Atmospheric Aerosol Loading

We are operating outside the conditions that supported human civilization for the past 11,000 years — and the framework exists precisely so we can see that, name it, and act on it.

Notice the ozone layer and aerosol loading — both sit inside the safe zone. Global cooperation in the 1980s, through the Montreal Protocol, brought ozone back from the edge. It is the clearest example we have of a boundary actually being pulled back. It is also proof that systemic change is possible.

The planetary boundaries framework is not a counsel of despair. It is a systems map. And systems maps tell you where to look, what to connect, and what levers might matter. That is precisely what we need educators to be able to do.

Connect the frameworks

Where do you see CAS in this picture?

You learned six properties of complex adaptive systems in Section 2: feedback loops, emergence, non-linearity, interdependence, adaptation, and tipping points. The planetary boundaries framework is not just a list of nine things — it's a systems map.

Pick one CAS property and explain where you see it operating in the planetary boundaries framework. A sentence or two is enough.

This is for your own thinking — not submitted. You may want to bring it to class.

  • Feedback loops → think about ice, oceans, and warming
  • Interdependence → think about freshwater, land-use change, and biodiversity all being crossed together
  • Tipping points → think about what "crossing" a boundary actually means
  • Emergence → think about whether anyone designed the current state of the planet
